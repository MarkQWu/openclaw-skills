# Overseas Compliance And Cultural Risk

Purpose: keep overseas legal, IP, and cultural risk separate from domestic-platform compliance. Domestic `/合规` rules may inform general safety thinking, but they do not define the overseas target-market standard.

## Hard Gates

Block planning, drafting, export, or publishability advice when any of the following is true:

1. **Target market missing**: the project says "overseas" but does not name a target market, platform, or audience assumption.
2. **IP exposure**: character names, family names, fictional organizations, signature scenes, dialogue, visual motifs, or episode sequences are traceable to existing dramas, novels, films, celebrities, brands, or public figures.
3. **Source-market transplant**: a domestic legal, family, hierarchy, workplace, school, medical, or status mechanism is used as if the overseas audience already understands it.
4. **Culture or protected-class harm**: race, religion, nationality, disability, gender, sexuality, or region is used as a shortcut for villainy, comedy, inferiority, or exotic texture.
5. **Consent/violence breach**: intimacy, coercion, threat, or violence violates the declared target-market relationship grammar in `hard-rules.md`.
6. **Real-world sensitive institution misuse**: police, court, hospital, military, immigration, religion, or school systems are used with specific claims that are unverified or target-market-inaccurate.
7. **Traceable source adaptation**: the premise, scene sequence, signature reveal, dialogue turn, title logic, or character constellation can be reverse-traced to a Chinese short drama, English novel, film, series, celebrity persona, or platform hit.
8. **Synthetic likeness exposure**: AI-generated faces, voices, gestures, names, or character packaging are traceable to real actors, influencers, public figures, or protected performance identities.

## Review Required, Not Automatic Block

Flag for human review when:

- A platform, brand, song, app, school, city, landmark, celebrity, public office, or real organization appears by name.
- The story relies on immigration, inheritance, custody, medical diagnosis, criminal procedure, marriage law, or estate law.
- The project targets a region with strong religious, political, or historical sensitivities.
- The material uses mafia, cartel, trafficking, kidnapping, stalking, forced proximity, or dubcon-adjacent romance grammar.
- Any source drama, novel, film, influencer persona, or competitor title was used as a reference during ideation.
- A generated image, face claim, voice, or character board was produced from a real-person reference.

## Known Similarity And IP Failure Patterns

### Scene-For-Scene Copying Is A Delivery Killer

ReelShort plagiarism scandal source cluster (2025-07): scene-for-scene copies of Chinese originals were flagged by DramaBox parent company and two more studios. Eight titles were flagged, three removed, and the legal status remained unresolved in the source note.

**Implication**: "Take a Chinese hit and translate it" is a legal and platform-delivery time bomb in Western jurisdictions. Overseas mode must generate **original content**: no adaptations of Chinese IP, no echoes of English IP, and no reusable signature scenes from known titles.

### Similarity Is Broader Than Names

Treat the following as IP/similarity exposure even when all names are changed:

- Same episode sequence or paywall reveal order.
- Same signature humiliation, rescue, oath, tattoo, ring, pregnancy, inheritance, or identity reveal.
- Same family constellation, rival arrangement, or mentor/betrayer function.
- Same opening image, trailer image, caption hook, or title promise.
- Same line turn, catchphrase, vow, threat, or confession shape.

### AI Likeness Risk

The source note on AI-generated short dramas included 670 pulled titles for unauthorized likenesses. The craft implication lives in `anti-domestic-transfer.md`; the compliance implication is here:

- Do not build characters from real actors, celebrities, influencers, or public figures.
- Do not use "looks like X" as a stable design instruction.
- Do not preserve a real person's face, voice, style signature, name pattern, relationship history, or public scandal in fictional packaging.
- When visual references are needed, describe original traits by function: age range, power signal, wardrobe logic, posture, and camera readability.

## Originality Test

Before export, ask:

1. Can a competitor, reader, author, or studio name the likely source?
2. Would a side-by-side scene comparison show the same sequence of actions?
3. Does the project depend on a known title's signature promise or twist?
4. Are any generated visual or voice assets traceable to a real person?

Any "yes" blocks export until the premise, sequence, character package, and visual identity are rebuilt.

## Target-Market Questions

Before treating a rule as universal, answer:

1. Which market is this for: US, UK, Canada, Australia, Southeast Asia, MENA, LatAm, Europe, or mixed English-language export?
2. Which platform grammar is assumed: ReelShort, DramaBox, ShortMax, FlexTV, GoodShort, or generic vertical drama?
3. Is the issue legal/IP, cultural interpretation, platform review, production feasibility, or reader expectation?
4. Does the risk block the premise, require a structural replacement, or only require line-level polish?

## Relation To Domestic Compliance

Domestic compliance files cover domestic platform red lines and may over-block overseas fiction if applied directly. For overseas mode:

- Use this file for target-market risk classification.
- Use `hard-rules.md` for genre-specific consent, violence, and agency boundaries.
- Use `platform-knowledge.md` only for sourced platform facts, not cultural assumptions.
- Use domestic `compliance-checklist.md` only as a fallback checklist for general safety topics, never as the governing overseas authority.
