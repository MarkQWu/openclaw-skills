## Governing Priority

Dialogue polish for overseas vertical short drama follows this order:

1. **Platform-native rules first**: use `dialogue-platform.md` for DramaBox / ReelShort / peer-platform line shape, legibility, trope grammar, VO expectations, and melodrama threshold.
2. **General craft second**: use the McKee / Mamet / Goldman / Seger / Scriptnotes principles in this file to make each line active, compressed, and character-specific.
3. **Long-form exemplars last**: use `dialogue-exemplar-risk.md` only as auxiliary technique, counterexample, or risk map. Peaky Blinders, The Godfather, Succession, The Wire, Game of Thrones, The Fall, Happy Valley, and similar prestige / long-form references are not platform norms.

If a prestige-TV or general-theory technique conflicts with peer-platform evidence, **platform evidence wins**. If a McKee / Mamet / Sorkin / prestige exemplar line feels more elegant but becomes less glance-legible, less trope-forward, or less emotionally explicit under mobile viewing conditions, it is a craft failure for this format.

## Methodology Foundation — General Craft Only

These principles are reusable craft tools. They do not override platform-native conventions.

### 12 Core Principles

1. **Dialogue is verbal action, not conversation.** Every line is a character doing something: persuading, evading, wounding, testing, refusing, recruiting, or surrendering. If no action verb is behind a line, it is "said" but not "done." (McKee, Mamet)
2. **Characters speak to get what they want, not to inform the audience.** Exposition-because-audience-needs-it is the default failure. In vertical short, necessary exposition must ride inside conflict, VO, threat, seduction, transaction, or reveal.
3. **Subtext is useful only when the viewer can decode it under platform conditions.** McKee's said / unsaid / unsayable model remains useful, but Seger's opacity-collapse principle governs this format: subtext too deep for first-time mobile viewing is opacity, not sophistication.
4. **Mamet's counter-position matters.** Plain action often beats writerly layered subtext. Write the simplest line that accomplishes the scene action; let situation and power differential carry the extra meaning.
5. **Every scene is a negotiation of power and want.** If the writer cannot name what each character wants from the listener in the exchange, the dialogue drifts.
6. **Character voice emerges from worldview, not vocabulary tricks.** Dialect, slang, and tics are the shallowest layer. True voice is what the character notices, values, refuses, fears, or assumes.
7. **Economy: cut until the line still lands, then test one more cut.** Shorter only helps when it stays legible. Platform compression is not cryptic prestige compression.
8. **Enter late, leave early.** Cut greetings, throat-clearing, and post-resolution talk unless the platform trope requires ritual wording.
9. **Dialogue reveals character under pressure.** Comfort dialogue conceals; pressure dialogue exposes hierarchy, fear, appetite, and boundary.
10. **No two characters want the same thing the same way.** Same-want plus same-strategy creates redundant speech.
11. **Dialogue responds to the immediately preceding pressure.** Meisner-style repetition is useful when a line reacts to what was actually said or done, not to the writer's outline.
12. **Silence is a line only when the shot can carry it.** Refusal to speak can be dramatic action, but vertical short rarely has runway for prestige pauses. Use silence only when a single reaction, physical action, or captioned context makes the beat unmistakable.

### Compression Techniques

- **Strip modifiers**: adjectives and adverbs are usually the writer speaking, not the character.
- **Prefer direct syntax under platform pressure**: SVO lines are easier to caption and parse at a glance.
- **Use fragments only when decoding is instant**: dominant characters can fragment, but cryptic fragments without visual anchors become opaque.
- **Replace exposition with implication when the platform supplies the key**: if the key is absent, use overt trope grammar instead.
- **Single-word response**: can be a power move, but needs clear question context and visible hierarchy.
- **End on the loaded word**: restructure so the operative word lands last when it improves caption impact.
- **Name-as-weapon / name-as-intimacy**: using, withholding, or repositioning a proper name can compress hierarchy.
- **Interrupt and overlap**: useful for speed and conflict, but burned-in captions may punish heavy overlap.

### Subtext vs. On-The-Nose

- **McKee**: subtext is default; on-the-nose is craft failure except in specific conditions such as crisis mask-drop or psychologically simple characters.
- **Mamet**: layered subtext can become writerly self-indulgence; plain action plus situation creates subtext automatically.
- **Seger, governing for vertical short**: subtext works only when the audience can decode it from available context. If the audience lacks the situational key, subtext collapses into opacity.
- **Platform reconciliation**: on-the-nose emotional and trope language is often correct in overseas vertical short when it preserves glance-legibility, immediate stakes, and paid-platform retention.

### Dialogue Economy for Constrained Runtime

1. **One want per scene, one want per line.**
2. **Cut greeting and goodbye unless the ritual itself is the conflict.**
3. **Every line should advance plot, reveal character, or build tension; strong lines do two of three.**
4. **Exposition must be carried by conflict, transaction, seduction, threat, VO, or reveal.**
5. **First and last lines of a scene carry disproportionate memory weight.**
6. **Caption legibility is a craft constraint, not a distribution afterthought.**

### Character Voice Differentiation

- **McKee**: voice = worldview + vocabulary + rhythm, in that order.
- **Mamet**: voice = want + disciplined refusal to embellish.
- **Seger**: cover-the-names test. Remove attributions; speaker should remain identifiable through sentence length, question-vs-statement ratio, concrete-vs-abstract words, and power posture.
- **Sorkin**: rhythm and cadence can differentiate voice, but music must not reduce platform clarity.

### Silence / Not-Speaking Principle

A character should not speak when:

- speaking would dissipate tension the scene needs,
- withholding is the power move,
- the truth is unsayable and a lie would weaken the character,
- the physical action or shot communicates the beat faster than speech.

Vertical short constraint: silence must read in a single visible beat. Multi-second prestige restraint belongs in `dialogue-exemplar-risk.md` as a risk pattern, not as a default method.

## General Craft Audit Checklist

Run after platform checks, not before them.

1. **Platform precedence test**: does the line obey `dialogue-platform.md` for this function?
2. **Action test**: name the verb. If the verb is only "inform" or "explain," revise.
3. **Want test**: name what the speaker wants from the listener in this exchange.
4. **Covered-name test**: remove attributions; can the speaker still be identified?
5. **Density test**: for each word, ask if removal breaks force or clarity.
6. **Last-word test**: is the operative word in the strongest caption position?
7. **Subtext-vs-opacity test**: first-time viewer, no rewind, small screen; do they get the emotional beat?
8. **On-the-nose earnedness test**: if direct emotional statement, is it platform-correct or earned by prior concealment?
9. **Silence test**: would not speaking be stronger, and can the shot carry it instantly?
10. **Exemplar contamination test**: did a prestige / long-form reference override platform grammar? If yes, revert to platform-native line shape.

## Genre Traditions Needing Line-Level Care

These are genre constraints, not platform proof. Validate them against `dialogue-platform.md` before use.

- **Mafia**: honorifics and hierarchy terms matter for American audiences: Don, consigliere, made man, sitdown, capo, underboss. See `hard-rules.md`.
- **Dark romance**: consent grammar timeline matters. Early dubcon / possession language must not erase required later explicit choice. See `hard-rules.md`.
- **Campus-set thriller with institutional power**: class markers need fast legibility without overwriting. Avoid novel-register density unless the platform scene gives an immediate visual key.

## Polish Pass Workflow

1. Identify the line function: VO, threat, transaction, reveal, possession, refusal, order, report, consent beat, or power flip.
2. Check `dialogue-platform.md` for expected platform-native wording, word count, and explicitness.
3. Apply this file's general craft audit to remove drift and strengthen action.
4. If borrowing a long-form technique, check `dialogue-exemplar-risk.md` and document why the technique survives platform constraints.
5. For any line failing opacity-collapse, replace elegance with platform-overt clarity.
