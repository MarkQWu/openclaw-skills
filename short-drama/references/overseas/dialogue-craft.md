# Dialogue Craft Reference — L1 / L2 / L3 Combined

> Created 2026-04-14 from three parallel research agents during EP1 polish pass prep.
> Purpose: durable reference for EP2-60 dialogue polish. Reusable across episodes.
> Structure: L1 (authoritative methodology) → L2 (genre traditions) → L3 (exemplars).
> **L3 validates against L1, not the reverse.** If an L3 technique doesn't execute an L1 principle, it's show-specific idiosyncrasy not portable craft.

## L1 — Methodology foundation (McKee / Mamet / Goldman / Seger / Scriptnotes)

### 12 core principles

1. **Dialogue is verbal action, not conversation.** Every line is a character *doing* something — persuading, evading, wounding. If no action verb is behind a line, it's "said" not "done." (McKee, *Dialogue* opening thesis; Mamet *On Directing Film*)
2. **Characters speak to get what they want; not to inform the audience.** Exposition-because-audience-needs-it is the cardinal failure. Find visual dramatization or leave it unknown. (Mamet; McKee *Story*)
3. **Subtext is the true content; text is the surface.** McKee's "said / unsaid / unsayable" three-layer model. Unsayable drives best scenes. (McKee *Dialogue*; Stanislavski)
4. **Mamet's counter-position**: uninflected, no "acting" on the page. Write the plainest line that accomplishes the action. Let situation create subtext. **This is direct disagreement with McKee** — flag when resolving.
5. **Every scene is a negotiation of power/want.** If you can't name what each character wants in this exchange, the dialogue drifts. (McKee; Ball, *Backwards and Forwards*)
6. **Character voice emerges from worldview, not vocabulary tricks.** Dialect/slang/tics are the shallowest layer. True voice is what the character *notices, values, refuses to say*. (McKee *Dialogue* Part 3)
7. **Economy: cut until it bleeds, then cut one more word.** Shorter = more weight per word. Maximum meaning, minimum words. (Goldman *Adventures*; McKee)
8. **Enter late, leave early.** Start after the greeting, end before the resolution. (Goldman; universally taught)
9. **Dialogue reveals character under pressure.** "True character is revealed in the choices a human being makes under pressure." Dialogue in comfort conceals. (McKee *Story* Ch. 5)
10. **No two characters want the same thing the same way.** Same-want = redundant character. Differentiation of want = differentiation of speech.
11. **Repetition principle** (Meisner): dialogue should respond to what was *actually* said/done, not to a planned line. Feel over outline.
12. **Silence is a line.** Refusal to speak = dramatic action. The beat = decision unit. (Mamet, Pinter, McKee)

### Compression techniques (short-loaded line mechanics)

- **Strip modifiers** — adjectives/adverbs are usually the writer speaking, not character
- **Uninflected declaratives** — "He's dead." not "He's... he's dead, oh god."
- **Fragment over complete sentence when the character is dominant or certain** — powerful characters don't establish grammatical completeness
- **Replace exposition with implication** — Seger: dialogue should reference backstory, not deliver it
- **Single-word response** — one-word answer to complex question = power move
- **End on the loaded word** — last word of a line is weight-bearing; restructure so operative word lands last (Sorkin)
- **Name-as-weapon / name-as-intimacy** — using or refusing a proper name is a compression mechanism
- **Interrupt and overlap** — characters talking over each other compresses two beats into one (Sorkin; Mazin *Chernobyl*)

### Subtext vs. on-the-nose — authorities disagree

- **McKee**: subtext is default; on-the-nose is craft failure *except* in specific conditions (crisis mask-drop, psychologically simple characters)
- **Mamet**: McKee-style layered subtext is writerly self-indulgence; write plain action, situation creates subtext automatically
- **Seger (middle position, most applicable to vertical short)**: subtext works when audience can decode from context; it collapses into opacity when audience lacks situational key. **The opacity-collapse principle is the governing L1 authority for vertical short format** — "subtext too deep for viewing conditions is craft failure, not sophistication."
- **When on-the-nose serves story (McKee's non-trivial answer)**: at the climax of a long-sustained concealment. Direct statement is earned by prior subtext, not avoided absolutely.

### Dialogue economy for constrained runtime (vertical short specifically)

1. **One want per scene, one want per line** — a line serving two purposes does the work of two lines (Ball)
2. **Cut the greeting, cut the goodbye** — enter late, leave early applied at line level (Goldman)
3. **Every line either advances plot, reveals character, or builds tension — ideally two of three.** Lines doing only one are cut candidates
4. **Exposition must be disguised as conflict** — in tight runtime, exposition rides on argument or seduction (McKee)
5. **First line and last line of a scene carry disproportionate weight** — 40%+ of viewer's memory of the scene

### Character voice differentiation (methodology differences)

- **McKee**: voice = worldview + vocabulary + rhythm, in that order of importance
- **Mamet**: voice = what character wants + refusal to embellish. Two characters with different wants sound different if writer stays disciplined on action
- **Seger**: the cover-the-names test — read scene without attributions, can you identify speaker? Diagnostics: sentence length per character, question-vs-statement ratio, concrete-vs-abstract word preference
- **Sorkin (documented practice)**: rhythm/music — each character has a meter; write aloud and listen for cadence differentiation

### Silence / not-speaking principle

Rule-level: a character should not speak when (a) speaking would dissipate tension the scene needs, (b) the power move is withholding, (c) the truth is unsayable and a lie would be beneath the character.

- Mamet: strongest dramatic moment is often refusal to respond
- McKee: the beat (measurable pause containing a decision) is a dialogue unit equal to speech
- Pinter: distinguishes pause (gathering) / silence (refusal) / unspoken (incapacity)

### L1 audit checklist (use for every EP line)

1. **Action test** — name the verb. If verb is "inform" or "explain," line fails
2. **Want test** — name what speaker wants from listener in this exchange; if can't, line is drift
3. **Covered-name test** — remove attributions; can voice be identified?
4. **Density test** — for each word, ask if removal breaks the line
5. **Last-word test** — is operative word last? If not, restructure
6. **Subtext-vs-opacity test (Seger)** — first-time viewer, no rewind, do they get the emotional beat?
7. **On-the-nose earned-ness test (McKee)** — if direct emotional statement, has prior concealment earned it?
8. **Silence test (Mamet/Pinter)** — would not speaking be stronger? If yes, cut the line

## L3 — Exemplar toolboxes (techniques validated against L1)

### Archetype 1: The heir who inherits violence (e.g., our Julian)

**Primary: Tommy Shelby, Peaky Blinders S1-2. Secondary: Michael Corleone, The Godfather.**

- **Word economy** — Tommy avg ~6 words/line in command mode; complete sentences for strategy (family-only), fragments for power (public/subordinates). Trigger: audience hierarchy.
- **Subtext density** — "I'll call you back" (Tommy S2) = closes scene + refuses rival agenda + asserts his clock + signals witness present. 4 jobs, 4 words.
- **Fragment rule** — any subordinate or public witness present → truncate. Family-only strategy → complete sentences.
- **Pre-name pause** — "…Arthur." The beat before the name does the scolding. **Caution: vertical short punishes >0.8s silences — compress pause technique but don't import 3s holds.**
- **Silence** — lights cigarette instead of answering threat; S1E2 Garrison scene pours drink instead of answering. Silence = "question does not rate reply."
- **L1 principles executed**: #1 (dialogue as verbal action), #3 (subtext via unsayable-in-front-of-wrong-audience), #7 (economy), #12 (silence as line)

### Archetype 2: The cornered woman who refuses to break (e.g., our Elara)

**Primary: Stella Gibson (The Fall), Catherine Cawood (Happy Valley). Secondary: Eve Polastri pre-compromise.**

- **Word economy** — Stella avg ~8 words under threat, drops to 2-4 when predator is physically present. Fragmentation = danger rising.
- **Subtext density** — "I'm not afraid of you." (Stella S2) = declaration + diagnosis (names his need) + refusal of game's premise. 3 jobs, 6 words.
- **Verb-first fragments** — survival speech fragments on verbs, keeps nouns. "Tomorrow. The river. Alone." — compressed itinerary, no emotional verb.
- **Factual-noun repetition over emotion-adjective amplification** — "He's seventeen. Seventeen." (Catherine) carries grief without word "grief."
- **"He chose me." (Stella S2) — direct L1 precedent for our "That's me. Tomorrow." architecture** — acknowledges threat's targeting + denies surprise + keeps clock.
- **Exit-as-line** — Happy Valley S1E1: Catherine walks away mid-predator's sentence. Exit is the line. **Caution: requires established baseline; not usable in EP1 before Elara has voice baseline.**
- **L1 principles executed**: #1 (action via verb-first position), #7 (economy), #9 (character-under-pressure reveal via form degradation)

### Archetype 3: The institutional retainer (e.g., our Marco)

**Primary: Varys, Game of Thrones S1-4. Secondary: Frank Vernon (Succession), Tom Wambsgans S1.**

- **Word economy** — Varys to Ned (S1) avg ~12 words, always complete sentences, always one comma. Retainers do NOT fragment; fragmentation = presumption.
- **Subtext density** — "I serve the realm, my lord. Someone must." (Varys S1E5) = loyalty declaration + correction of Ned's framing + mild rebuke + self-authorization. 4 jobs, 9 words, complete grammar.
- **Inverted grammatical rule** — retainers complete sentences *even when truncating content*. "As you wish, my lord" = full sentence carrying zero new data; completeness IS the deference. Fragment = scared or about-to-betray.
- **Conditional softeners** — "If I may…", "Were I to suggest…" **CAUTION: this technique eats 3 words — vertical short can't afford it. Use principal's-name-at-sentence-end as lighter alternative.**
- **Principal's name at sentence-end** (not start) — respect marker; inverted from Tommy's pre-name pause. End-position = respect. Start-position = warning.
- **Silence as presence-data-collection** — Varys standing S1 council scenes, Tom Wambsgans nodding. Not withholding; it's watchfulness.
- **Precedent for silent character in intro episode**: Luca Brasi (Godfather, ~20 words first act but present in 8 scenes). **Key: Marco must be visibly seen observing in multiple frames, not just absent.**
- **L1 principles executed**: #6 (voice from worldview — retainer worldview requires grammatical deference), #10 (differentiation via form)

### Archetype 4: The young soldier / field operator (e.g., our Tomaso EP2+)

**Primary: Michael Gray / John Shelby (Peaky Blinders). Secondary: Omar Little (The Wire).**

- **Word economy** — John Shelby reporting to Tommy avg 5-8 words, declarative. Noun-heavy, adjective-free.
- **Subtext density** — "It's done." (John S1) = closes task + reasserts competence + invites next order.
- **Reports = fragment, requests-to-principal = full grammar** — trigger for grammatical switch. "Two men. North gate. Armed." (report) vs "Do you want me to handle it?" (request).
- **One signature tic maximum** — Omar's courtly-register-mismatch ("Indeed."); John Shelby's half-laugh before bad news. **CAUTION: in 60+ hours of runway it works; in 137s, ≥2 tics = cartoon. Tomaso gets one, only if it pays off EP2+.**
- **Sonic/physical substitution** — Omar whistles instead of speaking during approaches. Whistle IS the dialogue.
- **L1 principles executed**: #1 (action via noun-stacking), #7 (economy), #10 (differentiation via voice form)

### Silence techniques (prestige precedents)

1. **Tommy lights cigarette instead of answering threat** (PB S1E2) — refusal-to-rate-question + time-claim + physical-readiness
2. **Stella holds phone silence before hangup** (The Fall S1) — denies predator his climax + asserts her clock + non-fear via breath control
3. **Varys standing silent at council** (GoT S1) — presence-as-intelligence-gathering + loyalty-ambiguity + position-refusal
4. **Luca Brasi in Godfather** — menace-without-verbalization + loyalty-via-physical + eventual-speech-rare-and-weighted
5. **Michael Corleone at restaurant before shooting** (~40s near-silence) — transformation-visible + lean-in + violence-earned-by-restraint

### Warnings (prestige techniques that DIE in 137s vertical)

- **Pre-name pauses >0.8s** — vertical punishes; compress to single-beat
- **Multi-scene retainer silence** — Varys gets 3 scenes, you get 1 frame. Silence must load on single reaction shot
- **Signature flourishes** ("Indeed.") — 60 hours of runway establishes; in 137s = affectation
- **Conditional softeners** ("If I may…") — eat 3 words vertical can't afford
- **Godfather restaurant 40s silence** — impossible at runtime
- **Exit-mid-sentence** — requires baseline the medium can't build in EP1
- **Novel-register monologue** — explicitly off-limits (see `memory/feedback_no_novel_references.md`)

## L2 — Platform conventions (DramaBox / ReelShort / peer platforms)

### 8 documented conventions (from 2024-26 platform research; confidence-tagged)

- **R1 [MED]** — Open on transaction, not atmosphere. EP1 starts *inside* the deal (contract / kidnapping / rejection), not before it.
- **R2 [HIGH for one verbatim anchor]** — FL motivation stated in VO within first minute. Verified example (Fatal Attraction): "Once I'm his lover, I'll have the power to avenge my mom!" No subtext.
- **R3 [MED]** — ML first appearance = display of power, not dialogue. Action first; first spoken line comes only after power differential is visually established.
- **R4 [HIGH]** — Trope-forward naming. Titles AND dialogue use "mafia king," "contract," "avenge," "own," "mine," "betrothed." No euphemism.
- **R5 [HIGH]** — Melodrama over subtext. Platform tone is "soapy, melodramatic, reverse-engineered" (John August). Prestige-TV restraint is a platform mismatch.
- **R6 [HIGH]** — Caption-legibility drives line writing. Burned-in captions are baseline; lines must parse in one glance — favors SVO, punishes ambiguous fragments.
- **R7 [MED]** — Mafia code-phrases are recycled not reinvented. "Born in Blood, Sworn in Blood" / "Enter alive and leave dead" (Bound by Honor) are genre shibboleths, not writerly lines.
- **R8 [LOW]** — Consigliere/lieutenant role often collapsed into ML's phone or visual henchmen; no strong named-retainer voice in peer EP1s surveyed.

### Platform-norm word count per function (inferred from R1-R6 + verbatim anchor)

| Function | Platform word-count range |
|---|---|
| FL text to sister | 2-6 words |
| ML phone ambient (business) | 4-10 words |
| ML phone-closer | 2-4 words |
| FL threat-awareness fragment | 6-14 words (with named stake) — **our "That's me. Tomorrow." is OUT of norm** |
| ML observation-as-demand | 2-5 words (typically name-directed not prop-directed) |
| FL power-flip | 5-12 words (typically explicit reveal, not cryptic) |
| ML consent-sliding idiom | 2-5 words (typically overt "You're mine" not polite "Drive carefully") |
| ML orchestration | 4-12 words, 2-3 beats |

### Risky patterns in prestige-derived writing

Patterns from L3 prestige exemplars that will FAIL platform norms:

- **Cryptic-fragment-as-threat** — peer platform states stakes plainly
- **Object-as-demand interrogation** — platform interrogates people, not props
- **Politeness-as-menace** — platform prefers overt possessive imperative
- **Abstract nouns in ML business-speak** — platform prefers concrete genre nouns (shipment, Russians, Thursday, auction)
- **Cryptic 2-word power flips by FL** — platform FL flips are reveals not riddles
- **VO absence** — single biggest structural deviation from platform R2

### Reconciliation with L1 (governing principle)

Platform conventions (R5 "melodrama over subtext") appear to conflict with L1 (McKee subtext-as-default). **Resolution via Seger opacity-collapse**: subtext is legitimate when decoding keys are supplied to the medium's attention conditions; subtext exceeding those conditions collapses to opacity = craft failure. Platform conventions are **consequences** of medium-attention conditions, not a separate aesthetic. Per-line application:

- Where visual anchor supplies decoding key in 3-second window → prestige compression valid
- Where no visual anchor → fall back to platform overt-trope grammar
- Test every line individually; don't adopt lane wholesale

## Using this reference for polish pass

1. For each scene, identify the 4 archetypes present (if any)
2. For each spoken line, run L1 audit checklist (8 tests)
3. For each prestige technique imported, verify L1 principle alignment
4. For each line failing Seger opacity-collapse test, adopt L2 platform-overt alternative
5. Document technique choice in the screenplay footer for future consistency
