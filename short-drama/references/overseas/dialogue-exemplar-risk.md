# Dialogue Exemplar Risk — Long-Form / Prestige References

> Split from `dialogue-craft.md` on 2026-05-15.
> Purpose: preserve useful long-form techniques while preventing them from becoming platform norms.

## Authority Boundary

Peaky Blinders, The Godfather, Succession, The Wire, Game of Thrones, The Fall, Happy Valley, No Country for Old Men, and similar references are **not** overseas vertical short platform standards.

They may be used only as:

- auxiliary technique sources,
- counterexamples,
- character-archetype inspiration,
- risk maps for what fails in 137-second vertical episodes.

They must never override `dialogue-platform.md`. Any imported technique must pass platform glance-legibility, trope explicitness, runtime, and caption tests.

## Archetype 1: The Heir Who Inherits Violence

Primary long-form references: Tommy Shelby in *Peaky Blinders* S1-2; Michael Corleone in *The Godfather*.

Useful techniques:

- **Word economy**: command-mode lines can be short and hard.
- **Subtext density**: a short line can close a scene, refuse a rival agenda, assert clock control, and signal a witness.
- **Fragment rule**: public / subordinate context may justify truncation; family-only strategy may allow fuller sentences.
- **Name as weapon**: a name placed after a beat can perform scolding or sentencing.
- **Silence as non-reply**: physical business instead of answer can signal "the question does not rate reply."

Platform risks:

- Pre-name pauses longer than a single beat punish mobile retention.
- Cryptic 2-4 word lines can become opaque without long-form context.
- Michael-style long restraint is not available at EP1 runtime.
- Prestige menace can underperform if the platform expects overt possession, threat, or transaction.

Allowed use: borrow economy and hierarchy. Do not borrow slow-burn mystique as a platform default.

## Archetype 2: The Cornered Woman Who Refuses to Break

Primary long-form references: Stella Gibson in *The Fall*; Catherine Cawood in *Happy Valley*; Eve Polastri before compromise.

Useful techniques:

- **Threat compression**: speech can shorten as physical danger rises.
- **Factual repetition over emotional adjectives**: concrete facts can carry grief or refusal.
- **Verb-first survival fragments**: itinerary / action fragments can show tactical control.
- **Exit-as-line**: leaving mid-sentence can be a power move when baseline is established.

Platform risks:

- "I'm not afraid of you" style restraint may be too understated if the platform scene needs a visible revenge / possession / survival hook.
- Exit-as-line needs established character baseline; it is weak in EP1 if the viewer has not yet learned her speech pattern.
- Ultra-compressed threat recognition such as "That's me. Tomorrow." may fall outside platform norm if the stake is not named.

Allowed use: borrow pressure-specific form degradation and factual repetition. Do not hide FL motive when the platform expects overt VO or reveal.

## Archetype 3: The Institutional Retainer

Primary long-form references: Varys in *Game of Thrones* S1-4; Frank Vernon and Tom Wambsgans in *Succession*.

Useful techniques:

- **Complete grammar as deference**: retainers may speak in full sentences even when content is withheld.
- **Conditional softeners**: "If I may..." and "Were I to suggest..." encode hierarchy.
- **Principal's name at sentence end**: respect marker; start-position can become warning.
- **Silence as data collection**: observing rather than withholding can define the retainer role.

Platform risks:

- Conditional softeners eat words vertical short cannot afford.
- A named retainer may not deserve EP1 line budget if platform peers collapse the role into phone / henchman / visual function.
- Multi-scene silent presence does not exist in 137-second runway unless the camera repeatedly marks the character.
- Succession-style institutional irony can confuse direct mafia / dark-romance hierarchy.

Allowed use: borrow grammatical deference and hierarchy markers. Do not overbuild retainer nuance before the platform hook is secure.

## Archetype 4: The Young Soldier / Field Operator

Primary long-form references: Michael Gray / John Shelby in *Peaky Blinders*; Omar Little in *The Wire*.

Useful techniques:

- **Report fragments**: "Two men. North gate. Armed." can compress field data.
- **Requests to principal use fuller grammar**: "Do you want me to handle it?" maps hierarchy.
- **One signature tic maximum**: a small sonic or register marker can differentiate.
- **Physical / sonic substitution**: whistle, knock, or phone buzz can replace a line.

Platform risks:

- Two or more tics in a short episode become cartooning.
- Courtly register mismatch needs long runway; in short vertical it may read as random.
- Noun-stack reports need clear visual anchor and immediate stake.

Allowed use: borrow report/request grammar switching. Do not import long-form eccentricity without payoff.

## Silence Techniques From Prestige Sources

- Tommy lights cigarette instead of answering threat: refusal-to-rate-question + time-claim + physical readiness.
- Stella holds phone silence before hangup: denies predator his climax + asserts her clock.
- Varys stands silent at council: presence-as-intelligence-gathering.
- Luca Brasi in *The Godfather*: menace-without-verbalization + loyalty through physicality.
- Michael Corleone before the restaurant shooting: transformation visible through restraint.

Platform translation:

- Compress silence to one visible beat.
- Pair silence with a clear physical action, reaction shot, or captioned context.
- Never spend long-form pause time unless the hook has already paid for it.

## Prestige Techniques That Often Die in 137s Vertical

- **Pre-name pauses longer than one beat**: mobile retention punishes them.
- **Multi-scene retainer silence**: Varys gets several scenes; vertical short may get one frame.
- **Signature flourishes** such as "Indeed.": long runway makes them iconic; short runway makes them affectation.
- **Conditional softeners** such as "If I may...": often cost three words with low platform return.
- **Godfather restaurant-length silence**: impossible at short-episode runtime.
- **Exit-mid-sentence**: requires baseline the medium often cannot build in EP1.
- **Novel-register monologue**: off-limits for platform dialogue unless explicitly adapted into screen-visible beats.

## Import Checklist

Use this before adopting any long-form exemplar technique:

1. **Source label**: which exemplar is being borrowed?
2. **Technique, not vibe**: name the specific mechanism, such as fragment report or name-as-weapon.
3. **Platform job**: name the platform function it serves.
4. **Glance proof**: confirm caption and visual context make it clear immediately.
5. **Runtime proof**: confirm it does not require long-form setup or silence.
6. **Replacement test**: compare with an overt platform line; keep the exemplar version only if it retains clarity and adds force.
